Design of Augmented Spark Igniter

August 22, 2018 by Garret Wiesehan, Jonathan Villegas

Like any good rocket engine, RPL’s “Remove Before Flight” needs a way to get itself fired up and running in a safe and reliable manner. Throughout the history of rocket design and development, a great deal of thought has gone into the method of initiating the controlled explosion that is the operation of a rocket engine. A wide variety of creative, complex, and ingeniously simplistic means of accomplishing this have been implemented. From spark plugs and lasers to hypergolic fuels which spontaneously ignite upon contact, ignition methods are diverse and selected upon the needs of the engine.

After careful examination we determined the need for a system that is robust, reliable, and can provide multiple engine starts as well as a challenge for our engineers. The engine that will power RPL’s entry into the Mars Society/FAR launch contest this May will contain an augmented spark ignition (ASI) system. This system utilizes the same LOX/LNG fuel that powers “Remove Before Flight” and is controlled and powered completely by onboard avionics and battery banks.

The overarching igniter design is somewhat of a simplified and miniaturized version of the rocket engine it is intended to ignite. WIth a small amount of impinging rocket fuel and a well-timed spark in a combustion chamber, a burning mixture of hot gasses is propelled into the engine’s thrust chamber. This method holds many advantages over more common methods of ignition. For example, many student liquid rocket engines are ignited by pyrotechnic methods; this involves placing essentially a small solid fuel rocket engine into a compartment in the main engine and setting it off with a electric filament. While this is a reliable method that generates a great amount of energy in a large area, the pyrotechnic charge must be replaced every time the engine is started and is quite sooty for the delicate instrumentations inside the engine. Spark ignition, which is another method in which a simple spark plug provides electric sparks to initiate combustion, does not provide as much energy nor is the flame dispersed. The drawback here is that such a localized source of ignition can increase the risk of a hard start, a destructive overpressure in the engine resulting from delayed ignition with excess fuel in the chamber or extreme asymmetries in initial ignition.

The ASI will soon undergo a series of tests at FAR under a variety of conditions, and will be the first testing of any component in the design of Remove Before Flight.Testing will assess the ASI’s performance over a range of mixture ratios, total fuel flow rates, operation duration, and spark duration to optimize operational parameters and future designs. It’s operation cycle is about one second, during which chamber temperature will reach up to 2500 °C. Measurements such as chamber pressure and temperature, LOX/LNG flow rates, and flame length will be recorded and logged. The data-driven analysis we receive from this test will be invaluable to the proper function of a critical component of our rocket and is a crucial first step in its development. Initial testing is expected to commence mid to late September with many more following shortly after.

Successfully implementing an ASI system in our engine represents a substantial challenge for us at RPL. The design choice demands more time, money, and careful engineering than simpler ignition methods. However, the advantages of safety, reliability, and repeatability that this system grants us greatly outweigh the difficulties that accompany it. The ASI is only one of several instances where RPL’s teams have selected the more challenging option to test our own capabilities as well as the capabilities of our rocket. In the meantime, we hope to provide exciting details and footage of our tests and progress before launch day!
